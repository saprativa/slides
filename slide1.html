The outline of today’s presentation is as follows. - I will start with a few basic concepts and terminologies used in the computational argumentation literature. - Then I will talk about some of the most significant classical argument diagrams That have been used in various fields such as logic, law and philosophy. - After that I will show you a handful of recent developments in the field of argument diagramming including argumentation knowledge graphs. - Then we will proceed to the multi-party meeting dialogue domain where we shall discuss about the diagrams that have been proposed specifically for capturing the meeting knowledge for computational processing. - Next I will describe our proposed schema and discuss the various issues that needs to be solved for arriving at the final schemas.

The basics

Joe Biden is the 46th President of the United States.

Joe Biden is the best President of the United States.

  • Let us start with a simple example of an argumentative vs non-argumentative statement.
  • This is an example of a statement which is objective. The readers or listeners may judge it as either true or false.
  • The second statement is subjective. In this case true-false judgments cannot be made. A person may either like or dislike the statement.
  • From this we can say that objective statements which state facts are generally non-argumentative. Arguments occur mostly in subjective sentences which express the views of the speaker or writer.

Historical diagrams

Whately (1836)

  • This is the earliest known use of an argument diagram. The book was published in 1836 but the first edition was published in 1826.
  • Whately was an English logician and Archbishop of Dublin.
  • He was the first person who used diagram to illustrate argumentative processes.
  • His method is to first figure out what is the ultimate conclusion and then trace the reasoning backward.
  • Whately wrote that the ultimate conclusion is proved by the two premises below it, grouped together. Then each premise in turn is proved by a separate group of premises that appears below it.
  • The final outcome is termed as a “chain of arguments” which is shown in this figure.

Wigmore (1913)

  • Wigmore was the first to use diagrams to map facts and inferential links in legal matters.
  • In this diagram the statement “Y died of poison” is the ultimate probandum.
  • Circle 7 is an interim probandum. Line connecting 7 to ultimate probandum means provisional probative force.
  • Wigmore’s main interest was in demonstrating the acceptability of the hypothesis given the factual evidence. Hence the upward direction. From evidence to hypothesis.

Beardsley (1950)

Beardsley represented the statements as nodes, using circled numbers, and he represented the links between the premises and conclusion as arrows joining the nodes. A piece of text can be transformed into an argument map using the following steps :

  • Separate statements by brackets and number them.
  • Put circles around the logical indicators.
  • Supply, in parenthesis, any logical indicators that are left out.
  • Set out the statements in a diagram in which arrows show the relationships between statements.

The example here is of a convergent argument.

Beardsley also provided examples of divergent and serial arguments.

We can observe that the arrows link premises to conclusions, no support is given to the inference between them.

Toulmin (1958)

The main revolution in the theory of argumentation was brought about by Toulmin in the late 1950s.

He was the first to take into consideration the defeasible generalization.

  • Claim: The conclusion of the argument or the statement the speaker wishes the audience to believe.
  • Data: The foundation or basis for the claim.
  • Warrant: The reasons behind the inference.
  • Backing: The support for the warrant.
  • Qualifier: The degree of force of the inferential link.
  • Rebuttal: Exceptions that might defeat the claim.

IBIS (1970)

IBIS stands for Issue Based Information System.

IBIS consists of three main elements:

  • Issues (or questions): these are issues that need to be addressed.
  • Positions (or ideas): these are responses to questions. Typically the set of ideas that respond to an issue represents the spectrum of perspectives on the issue.
  • Arguments: these can be Pros (arguments supporting) or Cons (arguments against) an issue. The complete set of arguments that respond to an idea represents the multiplicity of viewpoints on it.

Issue-Based Information Systems (IBIS) was originally meant to support coordination and planning of political decision processes.

More recently

Large Scale Argumentation Structure

Proposed two novel models by analyzing 5 biochemistry journal papers:

  • Claim graph
  • Model of Informational Hierarchy (MIH)

All work in this paper is manual.

Claims are categorized into three types:

  • Figure claims
    • Direct support from data in figures/tables.
  • Claim claims
    • Supported by other claims, or
    • Supported by other claims + figure/table (this is occasional)
    • Further three types:
      • Tertiary claim-claims
        • 1 or 2 supporting claims
      • Secondary claim-claims
        • 3 or more supporting claims
        • no relevance to the title/main concept of the paper
      • Main claim-claims
        • 3 or more supporting claims
        • Directly related to the main concept/theme of the paper
  • Others
    • Observations from experiments which could not be fitted to the data

In Meeting Domain

Meeting Description Schema

Pallotta et al. proposed Meeting Description Schema (MDS) as part of the IM2 project. (Interactive Multimodal Information Management - Swiss)

It is based on IBIS and XML.

Annotation schema consists of:

  • Dialog
  • Episode (new episode - topic change)
  • Turn (new turn - speaker change)

Here they have considered relations between episodes only.

The episodes could be:

  • Open
  • Agenda
  • Discussion
  • Close, etc.

Each episode can be composed of sub-episodes such as the Agenda episode here.

Categories such as AKS, ACCEPT, REJECT might correspond to dialogue acts.

They used MDS to annotate the ICSI corpus. (International Computer Science Institute, Berkeley)

Twente Argumentation Schema

Rienks et al. introduced the Twente Argumentation Schema which is a diagramming model for meetings.

This schema is inspired by the Rhetorical Structure Theory (RST).

Only those type of meetings are considered in which the participants discuss the pros and cons of solutions to problems which is somewhat related to IBIS model.

The schema was used to annotate the remote-control design meetings from the AMI meeting corpus.

The nodes:

  • Issues
    • Open issue
    • Yes/No issue
    • A/B issue
  • Statements
    • Weak statement
    • Statement
  • (Other)

The edges/relations:

  • Clarification/(Elaboration)
  • Specialization and Generalization
  • (Request)
  • Option
  • Option-exclusion
  • Positive
  • Negative
  • Uncertain
  • Subject-to

Conversation Graphs

Conversation graphs are diagrams that summarize:

  • What topics were discussed,
  • How long they were discussed,
  • Which participants were involved and
  • What type of arguments they contributed with.
